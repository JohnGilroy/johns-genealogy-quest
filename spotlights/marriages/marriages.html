Marriage records and customs

Marriage in Scotland

Regular and irregular marriage in Scottish records

Scottish marriage law differed markedly from that of England. The records reflect a legal tradition in which mutual consent mattered greatly, and in which both regular and irregular marriages could be recognised.

In brief

Until the mid-twentieth century, most people in Scotland married in a regular marriage, conducted by a minister after the publication of banns. Alongside this, however, Scottish law also recognised irregular marriage, in which a couple could form a legally valid marriage by mutual consent without the same formal church ceremony.

This helps explain why Scottish marriage evidence appears in several different forms: proclamation entries, parish register entries, kirk session minutes, sheriff warrants, and later statutory registration.

How marriage worked in Scotland

Marriage after proclamation

Until the mid-twentieth century, most people in Scotland were married by a minister in what was known as a regular marriage. This normally involved being proclaimed on three consecutive Sundays at the parish church before the ceremony itself took place.

These are the entries that often look most familiar to family historians: a couple publicly announced, then married under the oversight of the Church.

Consent made the marriage

Alongside regular marriage, Scottish law also recognised irregular marriage. In its simplest form, a man and a woman could make a declaration before witnesses that they accepted one another as husband and wife. For that reason it was also known as a marriage by declaration.

By showing proof of the marriage, the parties could obtain a warrant from a sheriff or sheriff substitute to have the union registered by the local registrar. Churches objected to such marriages in principle, but often accepted them in practice for fear that couples would otherwise “live in sin”.

“The law of Scotland as to marriage was this – it adopted the principle that consent alone made marriage… The law of Scotland did not require the presence of a priest, nor the intervention of any religious ceremony.”

Hansard, Registration of Births and Marriages (Scotland) Bill, August 1848

Rebuked for irregularity, then accepted

Before statutory registration began in 1855, some couples appeared before a kirk session acknowledging themselves to be husband and wife. The session minutes sometimes record that they were rebuked for their irregularity and absolved, and in some cases fined.

These records show the tension between legal recognition and religious discipline. The Church disapproved of the route by which the marriage had been formed, yet still dealt with the couple as married.

Forms of irregular marriage

Scottish law recognised several ways in which a valid irregular marriage could be formed or proved:

Per verba de praesenti was a present exchange of consent by both parties to be husband and wife. Ideally this was done before witnesses. A marriage without witnesses could still be legal, but it was much harder to prove.

Per verba de futuro subsequente copula was a promise of future marriage followed at a later time by sexual intercourse.

Marriage by cohabitation with habit and repute referred to a couple living together and publicly behaving as husband and wife. This was not so much a ceremony as a form of evidence that a marriage had taken place, based on reputation and conduct over time.

Why irregular marriage mattered

In Scotland, an irregular marriage, once proved, had the same legal consequences as a regular one. It could affect property rights, inheritance, and the claims of a widow and children on a husband’s estate.

As T. C. Smout observed, the Scots held to the simple doctrine that if two unmarried people of lawful age freely expressed their wish to marry and accepted one another in marriage, then they were married. Consent made the marriage, not the clergy or the civil official.

This helps explain why such marriages remained legally valid until 1939, even if they sat uneasily alongside the Church’s preferred practice.

A different system in England

The law of marriage in England developed along a more restrictive path. Lord Hardwicke’s Marriage Act of 1753 tightened the rules around valid marriage, and later reforms allowed marriages to take place either in church or before a registrar, but within a far more formal legal framework.

In Scotland, by contrast, mutual consent remained at the heart of the law for much longer. That difference is one reason why Scottish marriage records can look so unlike those found south of the border.

Why these records matter

The recent release of National Records of Scotland kirk session minutes on Scotland’s People has made it easier to explore how irregular marriage affected real lives and how the Church of Scotland responded to it. These records add colour and human detail to the legal framework, showing not only how marriages were recognised, but how communities reacted to them.

Together, parish registers, sheriff warrants, kirk session minutes, and later statutory registers reveal a distinctive Scottish marriage tradition. They show a legal system in which consent was central, yet one that also had to be explained, defended, and sometimes regularised in the face of religious disapproval.
