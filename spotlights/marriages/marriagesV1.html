Marriage records and customs

Ways People Could Get Married

From banns and licences to Scottish irregular marriage

Marriage was not always recorded in the same way. The surviving documents reflect changing law, church practice, and local custom.

In brief

Marriage records are not all alike. Some preserve the public reading of banns, some record a church ceremony, some reflect marriage by licence, and in Scotland some preserve the traces of irregular but legally recognised unions.

Understanding how a couple could marry helps explain why surviving records differ so much in wording, layout, and detail.

How marriages were recorded

The records below show that marriage could be approached in several different ways. Some entries preserve notice of an intended marriage, some record a church ceremony, and others reflect older Scottish legal traditions in which mutual consent mattered greatly.

Banns / Proclamation

Marriage after banns or proclamation

For many couples, marriage began with public notice. In England this was usually by banns, while in Scotland proclamations served a similar purpose. The intention to marry was announced publicly so that objections could be raised if necessary.

These records are often brief, but they are important because they remind us that marriage was a public legal process, not just a private ceremony.

Licence

Marriage by licence

Not every couple waited for banns. A marriage licence allowed the ceremony to take place more quickly, often for a fee. Such records can sometimes preserve useful details about age, residence, status, or consent.

For family historians, licences are often especially interesting because they may survive with related papers that provide context beyond the basic marriage entry.

Church ceremony

Scottish regular marriage

Many Scottish marriages were regular marriages, celebrated by a minister after proclamation of banns. These entries often look more familiar to modern researchers: the intended marriage was publicly announced, and the ceremony was then recorded in a parish register.

Irregular marriage

Scottish irregular marriage

Scotland also preserved older legal traditions that recognised marriage formed by mutual consent. A couple did not always need the same formal church process that would have been expected elsewhere. To modern eyes these marriages can seem informal, but they were part of a real legal framework.

This helps explain why Scottish marriage evidence can sometimes appear in forms that differ markedly from the standard parish marriage entry.

Contract / legal process

Contract, consent, and legal form

Older marriage law often treated marriage as a contract as much as a ceremony. The language of agreement, proclamation, and consent appears throughout the records and reminds us that marriage could involve a process rather than a single moment.

Civil registration

Statutory marriage registration

By the nineteenth century, marriage records became more standardised. Statutory registers generally provide fuller and more consistent detail, marking a shift from local custom to national administration.

Why Scotland stands out

To modern readers, Scottish irregular marriage can seem surprising. In reality, it reflects a legal tradition that gave great weight to mutual consent. A couple did not always need the same formal church process that would have been required in England.

As a result, the evidence for a marriage may appear in several different forms: proclamation entries, parish register entries, statutory records, kirk session material, or related legal documents. This makes Scottish marriage research especially interesting, because the records reveal both the law and the lived experience of ordinary people.

From custom to bureaucracy

Over time, marriage recording became more standardised. Earlier records often reflect local practice and legal tradition, while later statutory registers present a far more uniform official format. Together, they show how ideas about marriage evolved from community recognition and church oversight towards central record keeping.
