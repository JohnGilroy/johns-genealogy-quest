Background and sources

The 21× great-grandfather of Chris is believed to be Edward Plantagenet, better known as King Edward III of England.

The trail for this connection comes from a published compilation, The Plantagenet Roll of the Blood Royal (1911), which lists descendants of Edward III who were living at the time of publication.

Chris’s great-grandmother, Ada Orton, appears in the Mortimer–Percy section (Part 1, p. 319), which is why this line is worth exploring further — even if it didn’t lead to an invitation to King Charles’ coronation.

The Plantagenets were a royal dynasty who ruled England from the mid-12th century into the late 15th century. The name covers the direct royal line and, later, the Lancastrian and Yorkist branches that shaped much of late medieval English history.

Edward III (reigned 1327–1377) is associated with major developments in monarchy and warfare, including the early phases of the Hundred Years’ War. Because he had a large family and many descendants, connections to Edward III appear across a wide range of later noble and gentry lines.

How to read the chart: each row shows a couple, with the line continuing down the left-hand side through the direct line of descent. Spouses are shown alongside to help place each person in their family context.

This particular line runs through well-known medieval noble families (including the Mortimers, Percys, Cliffords and others) before it reaches later generations recorded in parish registers, civil registration and census returns.

It is also worth remembering that ‘descent from Edward III’ is not rare in Britain: because many generations have passed and Edward III had many descendants, a great number of modern families can connect through one or more routes. What matters here is documenting this specific route carefully.

Next steps for strengthening the evidence are to (1) verify each parent–child link with a primary record where possible, (2) ensure dates and places are consistent from generation to generation, and (3) cite modern scholarly compilations when medieval sources are involved.

The Plantagenet Roll is best treated as a guide rather than proof: it is indexed and often helpful for “joining the dots”, but it generally does not provide the detailed source citations that modern genealogy expects. Where practical, each generation in the chart should be confirmed against primary records and reliable modern scholarship. This page is a fun and motivating thread to pull — and a reminder that good genealogy is built one carefully sourced generation at a time.
