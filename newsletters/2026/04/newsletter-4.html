The Big Families

A light‑hearted look at the largest families in our family tree

Hello everyone,

One of the things that quickly becomes apparent when exploring older parish records and census returns is just how large families often were in the past.

It’s not unusual to come across households with ten or more children — something that feels extraordinary today but was far more common in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, particularly in rural communities.

This month’s spotlight takes a light‑hearted look at the most prolific parents in our family tree — the ancestors who appear to have had the largest number of recorded children.

The largest families in the tree

Topping the list is John Alexander, who had an impressive 15 children across two families in Ramsbury, Wiltshire.

Several other ancestors follow closely behind with thirteen or fourteen children — numbers that make modern family sizes seem positively modest.

Why were families larger?

There were several practical reasons why families were larger in earlier centuries.

First, infant and childhood mortality was much higher than today. Illness, poor sanitation, and limited medical care meant that not every child survived into adulthood. Having many children increased the chances that some would live long enough to start families of their own.

But large families were also economically useful, particularly in agricultural communities. Children helped with farm work, household labour, and local trades from a relatively young age.

Long before pensions or social support systems existed, children were often the main source of security in later life for their parents.

Interestingly, once people survived the dangerous early years of childhood, lifespans were often quite reasonable — something that becomes clear when tracing the later lives of many of these ancestors.

A glimpse of everyday life

Looking through the records, large families begin to feel less like a statistical curiosity and more like a window into everyday life: busy households, crowded cottages, and children growing up surrounded by siblings, cousins, and neighbours.
