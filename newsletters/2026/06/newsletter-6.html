Death and Ancestors

It’s worse than that, they’re dead, Jim

Hello everyone,

This month’s update has taken a slightly darker turn. Having looked recently at the largest families in the tree, it seemed only fair to give some attention to the other end of the story.

Death records are not always cheerful reading, but they are often some of the most revealing records we have. A few words on a certificate can tell us about illness, accidents, working conditions, uncertainty, and sometimes official investigations that happened after the first entry was made.

So, with a nod to Star Trek and a fairly predictable family sense of humour, this month’s spotlight is: “It’s worse than that, they’re dead, Jim.”

Death and correction in Scotland

The new spotlight looks at Scottish death records, especially the extra detail that can appear when a death was sudden, accidental, uncertain, or required official inquiry.

In Scotland, the original death register was not simply rewritten when more information came to light. Instead, the change was recorded separately in the Register of Corrected Entries, usually known as the RCE. A note was then added to the original register to point to the correction.

For family history, that can make a huge difference. A brief phrase such as “killed by being injured with wagons” can become a much clearer account of a colliery accident, with details of where it happened, how it happened, and whether there was an official inquiry.

Some causes make you pause

The spotlight includes several examples from the family tree, ranging from lingering illness to industrial accidents and sudden deaths in unexpected places.

Some of the medical language is familiar: bronchitis, pneumonia, cancer, and heart disease. Other phrases feel much more of their time: dropsy, phthisis, apoplexy, softening of the brain, and senile decay.

A few entries are blunt enough to make a modern reader wince. Others are moving because they show how little was known at the time, using words such as apparently, probable, or not certified.

There are also examples where the correction adds the missing human context: a death on a public footpath, a fatal accident at a colliery, or a hazardous substance that was part of everyday working life.

A new ancestor generation outline

The second new addition is less grim, although it does still involve quite a lot of people who are no longer with us.

I have added a new ancestor generation outline. This gives a generation-by-generation view of direct ancestors, starting close to the present and working backwards through the tree.

Each generation can be expanded to show ancestral couples, dates, places, and links through to WikiTree where available. It is designed as a clearer way to explore the direct lines without being overwhelmed by the full tree all at once.

It also highlights where the line currently ends, where information is missing, and where the family story moves beyond the usual counties into overseas connections.

Why this matters

The deaths spotlight and the ancestor outline are really two sides of the same project.

The ancestor outline helps show where each person fits in the family. The death records help show what happened to them, sometimes in very direct and vivid terms.

Together, they move the site beyond lists of names and dates. They help turn the tree into a set of connected lives: families, places, occupations, illnesses, accidents, uncertainties, and occasional eyebrow-raising register entries.

As ever, the research is still a work in progress. Some lines are well documented, some are still thin, and some are waiting patiently for me to find the next useful record.

If you spot a familiar name, remember a family story, or simply find a death record that deserves the phrase “they could have worded that more gently”, let us know.
