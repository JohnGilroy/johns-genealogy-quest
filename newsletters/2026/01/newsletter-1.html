Hello everyone,

Welcome to the first of what we hope will become an occasional family history newsletter.

Over the past few years we’ve been quietly researching our family tree — following certificates, census records, newspapers, photographs, and the stories passed down through the generations — and shaping them into something readable and shareable. This newsletter is a new way of sharing that work with family and friends who are curious about where we came from and the lives our forebears actually lived.

Along the way we’ve discovered that our family story contains just about everything. Some lives unfolded quietly; others were shaped by scandal, hardship, poor decisions, or sheer bad luck. But threaded through it all are resilience, creativity, and moments of real achievement. Our aim is not gossip, but context — to understand people as they were, through the evidence they left behind.

Who we are

John and Chris — putting the family history together

We’ve always been interested in where we came from and how ordinary lives intersect with wider events. What began as curiosity gradually turned into a long-running research project, pulling together records, newspaper reports, and family memories into a structured family history.

Together, we act as curators rather than storytellers for their own sake: following the records where they lead, and presenting what we find as clearly and respectfully as possible.

Our main family lines

To help you find your bearings, most of our research falls into four connected lines — two Scottish lines on John’s side, and two English/Welsh-border lines on Chris’s side.

Gilroy & Wilson (John’s Scottish lines)

John’s paternal and maternal Scottish lines largely begin with agricultural workers and domestic servants in Haddingtonshire, Kincardineshire and South Lanarkshire. Over time, many families moved into Scotland’s central belt as farming changed and industry mechanised — a familiar story of rural roots giving way to industrial towns and cities.

Reynolds & Law (Chris’s lines)

Chris’s dad

Chris’s mum

Reynolds: This line includes merchants, clergy and farmers from Yorkshire, Northumberland and Herefordshire, with many later based in London. It also contains our much-mentioned “royal connection” — traced through published pedigrees back to King Edward III — which is fascinating to explore, even as we treat it with a careful, evidence-first eye.

Law: Rooted in Gloucestershire and Wiltshire, this line includes generations of agricultural labourers. It also contains a dramatic chapter from the early nineteenth century, when family members were transported to Australia following protests connected to agricultural mechanisation — a stark reminder of how quickly social and economic change could upend ordinary lives.

Note: Living individuals are redacted on the public site. For our collaborative family tree (and to explore connections), we also maintain profiles on WikiTree.

What’s coming next

Future newsletters will be shorter and more focused, often drawing on a single story or theme — a newspaper discovery, an unusual life, or a wider historical moment that shaped several generations. Some content will appear on the website first; some will be written especially for the newsletter.

Finally…

If you have corrections, family stories, photographs, or memories you’d like to share, we’d genuinely love to hear from you. Family history works best when it’s collaborative.

Thank you for reading — and welcome.
