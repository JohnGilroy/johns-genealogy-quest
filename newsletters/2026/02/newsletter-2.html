Breach of the peace, sheep worrying and theft:

and what the papers chose to notice

Hello everyone,

Researching family history often reveals something quietly reassuring: the worries, disputes, and scandals that filled the newspapers a century ago are not so different from those we read about today.

This month’s selection of press stories — drawn from routine court reports and familiar rural concerns — shows how little has changed, especially when it comes to what catches the media’s eye and keeps readers talking. Each item below links to a short “spotlight” on the website.

A court reporter can’t resist the headline

Falkirk court report, 1931

A 1931 Falkirk court report involving my grandfather and uncle, William James Gilroy, proves that newspaper storytelling has always favoured the memorable over the mundane. While the charge itself was unremarkable, it was a moment involving “a glass eye and a pair of spectacles” that secured the headline — and ensured the story lingered long after the verdict.

Sheep worrying, not a new phenomenon

Gloucestershire court report, 1934

A Gloucestershire court case from 1934, involving Chris’s grandfather Albert Ernest Law, reminds us that dogs worrying sheep is far from a modern rural complaint. Long before today’s warning signs and public campaigns, livestock owners were already grappling with the serious consequences of uncontrolled dogs on farmland.

Impact of combat-related trauma

Sheffield court report, 1919

A court report from Sheffield in 1919 offers a glimpse of the difficult aftermath of war for returning servicemen. In a case involving my grandfather, William James Gilroy, the defence drew attention to delusions and violent mood swings linked to severe head injuries sustained during active service — language that strongly echoes modern understandings of combat-related trauma.
