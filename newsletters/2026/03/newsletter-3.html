Whaur are we fae?

A light‑hearted look at our most prolific counties of birth

Hello everyone,

One of the simplest — and most revealing — questions in family history is: Where are we actually from? (Not in the philosophical sense… just geographically.)

This month’s update spotlights the website’s new Origins Spotlights. They take a broad sweep through the recorded birthplaces in our family tree and ask a straightforward question: which historic county appears most often?

This isn’t a forensic boundary audit or a deep dive into spelling variations. It’s a snapshot based on what’s currently recorded — but it produces some satisfyingly clear patterns.

Can you guess the counties?

Rather than showing the full maps here, we’ve extracted the outline of the most frequently recorded historic county of birth from each of our family lines. Have a guess before reading on…

The reveal

For Chris’s family, the most prolific county of birth is Wiltshire.

For my side of the tree, it’s East Lothian — historically known as Haddingtonshire.

Which perhaps tells us something about parish stability, local work, and how long families could remain rooted in one area before the “pull” of new opportunities started shifting people around.

A word about the maps (choropleths!)

In the spotlights, the maps are presented as choropleth maps.

A choropleth map shades areas according to the frequency or intensity of a variable — here, the number of recorded births in each county. In plain English: the darker the county, the more of “us” were recorded there.

It sounds grand and statistical (and it is a common tool in demographic mapping), but in our case it’s simply a helpful way to make patterns visible without needing a spreadsheet and a strong cup of tea.

Historic and lieutenancy counties

One important note: the counties used in the spotlights are historic and lieutenancy counties, rather than modern administrative boundaries.

That’s because parish registers, censuses, and older civil records tend to use the traditional county names our ancestors would have recognised. Boundaries have shifted over the years — but the historic counties give us a more consistent frame of reference for family history research.

It’s also worth repeating: this is a snapshot, not a verdict. Spelling variations happen, boundary quirks exist, and some places sit awkwardly between “what the record says” and “what we’d call it today”. But even with those caveats, the clustering is unmistakable.
