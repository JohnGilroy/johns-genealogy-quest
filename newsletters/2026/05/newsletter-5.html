Marriage in Scotland

Why Scottish marriage records look so different

Hello everyone,

This month’s spotlight looks at something that sits at the heart of every family tree — marriage.

At first glance, marriage records can seem straightforward. But as soon as you begin working with Scottish records, it becomes clear that things were not always quite so simple.

In Scotland, marriage was not defined solely by a church ceremony. Instead, the law placed great weight on mutual consent, meaning that couples could be legally married in ways that feel unfamiliar today.

Regular and irregular marriage

Most people were married in what was known as a regular marriage, conducted by a minister after banns were read in the parish church.

Alongside this, however, Scottish law also recognised irregular marriage, where a couple could become legally married simply by declaring themselves husband and wife — sometimes in front of witnesses, and sometimes later confirmed through legal or church processes.

These marriages were not informal in the modern sense. Once proven, they carried the same legal weight as any other marriage, including rights of inheritance and property.

A glimpse into real lives

Some of the most interesting records come not from the marriage itself, but from what happened afterwards.

Couples who had married irregularly sometimes appeared before a kirk session, where they acknowledged their relationship, were formally rebuked for their “irregularity”, and then accepted by the Church.

These records offer a glimpse into how communities balanced law, religion, and everyday life — and how ordinary people navigated those boundaries.

Why it matters for family history

Understanding how marriage worked in Scotland helps explain why records appear in so many different forms.

A marriage might be found in a parish register, a sheriff’s warrant, a kirk session minute, or later in a statutory register — each telling a slightly different part of the story.

What can seem confusing at first becomes much clearer once you realise that consent, not ceremony, lay at the heart of Scottish marriage law.
